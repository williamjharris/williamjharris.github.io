Bone Mechanical Forces — Case Study Analysis

Data visualization and analysis of a landmark case study on long-term skeletal adaptation to mechanical loading — examining whether bone benefits from youth physical activity persist into the tenth decade of life.

Spring 2026 — BMEN 2010
Biomechanics Bone Biology pQCT DXA Case Study
Annotated Case Study Source Paper (Warden & Mantila Roosa, 2014)

Overview

This case study (Warden & Mantila Roosa, 2014, J Musculoskelet Neuronal Interact) examines a 94-year-old former MLB pitcher who threw left-handed professionally but was right-hand dominant in all other activities. This unique setup allowed researchers to isolate the long-term skeletal effects of throwing-related mechanical loading from simple arm dominance — providing a rare within-subject controlled look at bone adaptation across nine decades of life.

Using peripheral quantitative computed tomography (pQCT) at the midshaft humerus, the study compared cortical bone mass, size, and estimated strength between his throwing and nonthrowing arms after 55 years of retirement.

Subject Characteristics

94 Age at assessment (years)
33 Total years throwing
55 Years since retirement
1,776 Professional games played

Key Findings

LOST — Cortical Bone Mass & Thickness

Active MLB players show ~50% greater cortical BMC in their throwing arm. In the subject, this advantage had fully reversed — his throwing arm had 1.4% less cortical BMC than his nonthrowing arm, matching controls. Likely driven by accelerated intracortical remodeling (medullary expansion +22.1%, trabecular infiltration +9.4%).

RETAINED — Bone Size & Estimated Strength

The throwing arm retained 14.3% greater total cross-sectional area and 15.2% greater polar moment of inertia (torsional rigidity) than the nonthrowing arm — despite the nonthrowing arm being dominant. Because bone mechanical strength scales with the fourth power of radius, distributing the same amount of bone material further from the central axis preserved meaningful strength advantages.

MECHANISM — Periosteal vs. Endocortical Surfaces

Age-related bone loss occurs primarily at the endocortical (inner) surface, not the periosteal (outer) surface — and the periosteal surface actually expands slightly with aging. Bone size gains deposited on the periosteal surface during youth are therefore shielded from age-related resorption, explaining why size and strength benefits persist while mass benefits do not.

Biomechanical Significance

This case has direct implications for bone health across the lifespan. Physical activity during growth — particularly before and during the adolescent growth spurt — preferentially deposits bone on the periosteal surface, increasing bone diameter. Even if mass is lost with aging, larger bones distribute remaining material more efficiently, maintaining fracture resistance into old age. The polar moment of inertia (a measure of torsional rigidity) scales with r⁴, meaning even modest increases in bone radius confer substantial mechanical advantages.

From a clinical standpoint, these findings reinforce that peak bone mass achieved in youth is not the only relevant metric for lifelong fracture risk — bone geometry, particularly cross-sectional size, may be equally or more important for skeletal resilience in old age.
